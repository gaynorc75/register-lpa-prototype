Overview

A lasting power of attorney (LPA) is a legal document that allows you (as 'the donor') to choose one or more trusted people ('attorneys') to act and make decisions on your behalf.

This guide is also available in Welsh (Cymraeg).

Unlike a will, an LPA sets out what decisions your attorneys can make while you're alive.

After an LPA has been registered, it can usually only be used when you've lost mental capacity. You can chose to let your attorney make property and financial decisions on your behalf as soon as it's registered.

You can cancel the LPA or remove certain attorneys at any time, whether it's been registered or not, as long as you have the mental capacity to do so.

The LPA ends when you die.

Your attorneys cannot use your LPA to make changes in your will.

There's a different process in Scotland and process in Northern Ireland.

There are 2 types of LPA:

  • personal welfare, for health and care decisions
  • property and affairs, for financial decisions

You can choose to make one type or both.

Why you might make an LPA

An LPA gives you more control over what happens to you if you have an accident or an illness and cannot make your own decisions (you 'lack mental capacity').

If you do not have an LPA and lose the mental capacity to make your own decisions, someone else will need to apply to a court to become your deputy.

Making an LPA instead of having a court appointed deputy:

  • may be quicker
  • costs less money
  • gives you more control
